\begin{EnglishAbstract}
	
	Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD) is one of the most common and severe genetic muscular disorders in children. It is characterized by progressive weakness of skeletal muscles, which gradually leads to reduced motor abilities and difficulties in performing daily activities. Accurate, quantitative, and repeatable monitoring of motor function in patients with DMD plays an important role in assessing disease progression, evaluating the effectiveness of therapeutic interventions, and designing rehabilitation programs. Currently, one of the commonly used tools for assessing motor function in these patients is the North Star Ambulatory Assessment (NSAA), which assigns a discrete score from zero to two to each motor task based on clinical observation and the therapist’s judgment. Despite its widespread use, the dependence of this scale on human assessment and the semi-quantitative nature of its scoring may lead to inter-rater variability and reduced repeatability of the results. The aim of this study is to design and develop a system based on inertial measurement sensors and machine learning algorithms for estimating NSAA scores. In this regard, a set of inertial sensors, including accelerometers and gyroscopes, was mounted on key body segments, and linear acceleration and angular velocity data were recorded during the execution of selected NSAA tasks. The collected raw data were subjected to preprocessing steps, including noise reduction, digital filtering, and temporal normalization where required. Subsequently, a set of statistical and time-domain features was extracted from the motion signals to prepare a structured dataset for further analysis and classification. To predict the scores of the motor tasks, machine learning algorithms were employed, and the performance of the models was evaluated using metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, specificity, and F1-score. In addition, during the preliminary phase of the study, field visits and observations of the motor status of several patients with Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy were carried out in collaboration with the clinical team, which contributed to a better understanding of movement patterns, functional limitations, and practical requirements for system design. The results indicated that the proposed approach, based on inertial sensors and machine learning algorithms, can serve as a complementary tool alongside clinical assessment and may contribute to improving the objectivity, accuracy, and repeatability of the scoring process. In the future, the proposed system can be used as a foundation for longitudinal patient monitoring, remote assessment development, and support for clinical decision-making.
